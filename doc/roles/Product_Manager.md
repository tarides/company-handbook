# Product Manager

## Role Overview

The Product Manager (PM) serves as the strategic and
operational hub between business stakeholders, customers, and
the engineering team. They are responsible for defining *what*
gets built and *why*, ensuring that the product delivers
measurable value to users while advancing company objectives.
The PM owns the product vision and roadmap, and is ultimately
accountable for the success of the features and initiatives
under their purview.

---

## Responsibilities

### Strategy & Vision

- Defines and maintains a clear product vision aligned with
  company goals and market opportunity.
- Conducts market research, competitive analysis, and user
  research to identify opportunities and threats.
- Translates high-level business objectives into a coherent
  product strategy.

### Roadmap & Prioritization

- Owns and maintains the product roadmap, balancing short-term
  delivery with long-term strategic goals.
- Prioritizes features, bugs, and initiatives based on data,
  customer feedback, and business impact.
- Makes and communicates trade-off decisions clearly to
  stakeholders.

### Requirements & Discovery

- Collaborates with customers, sales, support, and internal
  stakeholders to surface and validate product needs.
- Authors clear, detailed product requirements documents and
  user stories with well-defined acceptance criteria.
- Owns the product backlog and ensures it is groomed,
  prioritized, and ready for engineering at all times.

### Cross-Functional Collaboration

- Acts as the primary liaison between engineering, design,
  marketing, sales, and leadership.
- Partners with UX/design to ensure features are intuitive,
  accessible, and meet user needs.
- Works with engineering leads to assess feasibility, scope,
  and delivery timelines.
- Aligns with marketing and sales on go-to-market strategy,
  positioning, and launch readiness.

### Delivery & Execution

- Participates actively in agile ceremonies (sprint planning,
  standups, retrospectives, reviews).
- Removes blockers and makes timely decisions to keep delivery
  on track.
- Monitors development progress and proactively flags risks or
  scope changes.

### Launch & Go-to-Market

- Defines launch criteria and coordinates readiness across all
  teams ahead of release.
- Ensures documentation, training materials, and support
  resources are prepared for new features.
- Communicates release notes and product updates to internal
  and external audiences.

### Metrics & Outcomes

- Defines clear success criteria for each feature or
  initiative before work begins.
- Analyzes product usage data post-launch to evaluate impact
  and inform next steps.
- Drives a continuous improvement loop: measure, learn,
  iterate.

---

## Key Skills & Competencies

- Strong written and verbal communication; able to tailor
  messaging to technical and non-technical audiences.
- Analytical thinking; comfortable working with data and
  drawing actionable conclusions.
- Customer empathy and user-centered design mindset.
- Ability to influence without direct authority.
- Comfort with ambiguity and skill in structuring unclear
  problems.
- Familiarity with software development processes
  (agile/scrum preferred).

## Interfaces With

| Role | Nature of Interaction |
| --- | --- |
| Engineering | Daily collaboration on scope, feasibility, and delivery |
| UX / Design | Shared ownership of user experience and feature design |
| Engineering Manager | Alignment on team capacity, priorities, and risk |
| Sales & Account Management | Feedback loops on customer needs; input on roadmap |
| Marketing | Go-to-market coordination, messaging, and launch planning |
| Executive Leadership | Roadmap reviews, strategic alignment, and escalation |
| Customer Support | Bug reports, feature requests, and user pain points |

---

## What Success Looks Like

- The team consistently ships features that users adopt and
  that move key metrics.
- Stakeholders have a clear understanding of priorities and
  the reasoning behind them.
- Engineering and design spend minimal time on re-work due to
  unclear requirements.
- The product roadmap reflects a coherent strategy, not just a
  wishlist.
- Product decisions are grounded in data and customer
  evidence, not assumptions.

## Comparison with Software Engineering Manager

The Product Manager and the Engineering Manager are closely
paired roles that share joint accountability for a team's
output, but they own distinct domains.

| | Product Manager | Engineering Manager |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Primary focus** | Product strategy, priorities, and user outcomes | Team health, delivery, and engineering quality |
| **Owns** | *What* gets built and *why* | *How* the team works and *who* is on it |
| **Success measured by** | Feature adoption, user value, and business impact | Team performance, reliability, and retention |
| **Relationship to roadmap** | Defines and prioritizes the roadmap | Represents engineering capacity and constraints |
| **Relationship to engineers** | Collaborator and day-to-day partner | Direct people manager |

In well-functioning teams, the PM and EM operate as peers —
the PM ensures the team is working on the right things, while
the EM ensures the team is healthy and capable of delivering
them.
