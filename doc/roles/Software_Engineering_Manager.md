# Software Engineering Manager

## Role Overview

The Software Engineering Manager (EM) is responsible for the
health, performance, and growth of the engineering team. The EM
operates at the intersection of people leadership, technical
oversight, and cross-functional collaboration, and is
ultimately accountable for the team's ability to deliver
quality software consistently.

---

## Responsibilities

### People Leadership & Team Health

- Hires, onboards, and retains strong engineering talent.
- Conducts regular one-on-ones with each direct report to
  provide support, unblock challenges, and maintain open
  communication.
- Provides timely, constructive feedback and fosters a culture
  of continuous learning and psychological safety.
- Identifies growth opportunities for team members and
  develops individualized career progression plans.
- Manages performance, including recognizing strong
  contributors and addressing underperformance clearly and
  fairly.

### Delivery & Execution

- Owns the team's ability to deliver software reliably, on
  time, and at an appropriate level of quality.
- Monitors progress against commitments and proactively
  identifies and addresses risks to delivery.
- Ensures the team has clear priorities, adequate resources,
  and minimal unnecessary interruptions.
- Removes organizational and process blockers that slow the
  team down.
- Maintains appropriate visibility into team workload and
  capacity, and adjusts plans accordingly.

### Technical Oversight

- Maintains sufficient technical understanding of the team's
  systems to make informed decisions about staffing, scope,
  and risk.
- Ensures the team adheres to engineering standards and sound
  development practices.
- Partners with senior engineers and tech leads to identify
  and address technical debt and systemic risks.
- Supports sound architectural decision-making without
  necessarily making all technical decisions directly.
- Champions engineering quality, maintainability, and
  operational reliability as ongoing priorities.

### Process & Ways of Working

- Establishes and continuously improves the team's development
  processes (planning, review, testing, deployment).
- Ensures retrospectives and other feedback mechanisms result
  in meaningful, actionable improvements.
- Reduces friction in day-to-day workflows and shields the
  team from unnecessary process overhead.
- Promotes clear documentation practices and knowledge sharing
  within the team.

### Cross-Functional Collaboration

- Acts as the primary engineering point of contact for the
  Product Manager, UX, and other stakeholders.
- Participates in roadmap planning and ensures engineering
  constraints and capacity are accurately represented.
- Communicates team progress, risks, and decisions clearly to
  leadership and partner teams.
- Coordinates dependencies with other engineering teams and
  ensures smooth handoffs.

### Strategy & Organizational Contribution

- Contributes to broader engineering strategy, standards, and
  culture across the organization.
- Provides input on team structure, tooling, and processes at
  the department level.
- Represents the team's needs and perspective in leadership
  forums and planning discussions.

---

## Key Skills & Competencies

- Strong people management skills; able to coach, motivate,
  and develop engineers at varying levels.
- Clear and direct communicator; comfortable delivering
  difficult feedback and navigating ambiguity.
- Technically credible; able to engage meaningfully with
  engineering decisions without micromanaging.
- Skilled at balancing short-term delivery pressure with
  long-term team health and quality.
- Strong organizational awareness; understands how the team's
  work connects to broader company goals.
- Able to build trust across functions and influence decisions
  without direct authority.

## Interfaces With

| Role | Nature of Interaction |
| --- | --- |
| Engineering Team | Day-to-day leadership, coaching, and unblocking |
| Product Manager | Joint ownership of planning, prioritization, and delivery |
| UX / Design | Coordination on feasibility and implementation approach |
| Tech Lead / Senior Engineers | Shared oversight of technical quality and architectural direction |
| Other Engineering Managers | Cross-team dependency management and organizational alignment |
| HR / People Team | Hiring, performance management, and employee relations |
| Executive Leadership | Progress reporting, escalation, and strategic input |

---

## What Success Looks Like

- The team delivers reliably and maintains a high standard of
  quality without burning out.
- Engineers feel supported, challenged, and clear on how to
  grow in their careers.
- Stakeholders trust the team and have confidence in its
  commitments.
- Technical debt and systemic risks are visible, understood,
  and being actively managed.
- The team improves continuously — in skill, in process, and
  in the quality of its output.
- Strong engineers want to join the team and existing team
  members choose to stay.

---

## Comparison with Product Manager

The Engineering Manager and the Product Manager are closely
paired roles that share joint accountability for a team's
output, but they own distinct domains.

| | Engineering Manager | Product Manager |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Primary focus** | Team health, delivery, and engineering quality | Product strategy, priorities, and user outcomes |
| **Owns** | *How* the team works and *who* is on it | *What* gets built and *why* |
| **Success measured by** | Team performance, reliability, and retention | Feature adoption, user value, and business impact |
| **Relationship to roadmap** | Represents engineering capacity and constraints | Defines and prioritizes the roadmap |
| **Relationship to engineers** | Direct people manager | Collaborator and day-to-day partner |

In well-functioning teams, the EM and PM operate as peers —
the EM ensures the team is healthy and capable of delivering,
while the PM ensures the team is working on the right things.
