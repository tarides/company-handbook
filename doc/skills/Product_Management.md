# Product Management: Core Skills

Product management is the discipline of deciding what a software
product should do and why, and ensuring that the right things get
built, in the right order, to serve users and advance the business.

1. **Strategy & Prioritization** — Defining a product vision,
   building a roadmap, and making defensible decisions about what
   to work on and what to defer. Knowing how to say no clearly
   and without damaging relationships.

2. **Communication & Storytelling** — Writing well, presenting
   clearly, and being able to explain the "why" behind a decision
   to audiences ranging from engineers to executives to customers.

3. **Stakeholder Management** — Managing competing interests from
   engineering, sales, leadership, and customers without losing
   the trust of any of them. Knowing how to align people around a
   decision without requiring unanimous agreement.

4. **Discovery & Research** — Understanding users through
   interviews, usability testing, surveys, and behavioural data.
   Synthesizing findings into actionable insight rather than just
   collecting opinions.

5. **Requirements & Specification** — Writing clear, unambiguous
   requirements and user stories. Knowing how much detail is
   enough — not so little that engineering guesses, not so much
   that it becomes prescriptive about implementation.

6. **Data Analysis** — Reading product usage data, setting up
   meaningful measurements before a feature ships, and
   interpreting results honestly — including when they contradict
   expectations.

7. **Business Acumen** — Understanding revenue models, margins,
   and how product decisions connect to commercial outcomes. Being
   able to talk about the product in terms the finance and sales
   teams recognize.

8. **UX Sensibility** — Not the same as being a designer, but
   enough fluency in user experience principles to be a useful
   partner to design and to recognize when something will confuse
   or frustrate users.

9. **Market & Competitive Awareness** — Knowing the landscape the
   product operates in — what competitors are doing, where the
   market is heading, and what that means for priorities.
